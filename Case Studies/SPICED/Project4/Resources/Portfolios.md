# What is a UX/UI Portfolio?

### A portfolio is ✅:

- A design argument about why you
- A proof of process & thinking
- A glimpse into your style & values
- An invitation to interview you

### a Portfolio Is Not ❌:

- A Dribbble shot collection
- A PDF dump of your slides
- A linear timeline of everything you ever did
- A website without context or reflection

## Why Portfolios Matter:

- Your portfolio is your first impression
- It communicates who you are beyond the resume
- It is a filter, not a folder
- Recruiters spend 20-30 seconds on average scanning it
- It's a storytelling tool more than a project archive

![image.png](attachment:f5818e3f-56ef-4a3e-908f-b51731bbce12:image.png)

### Portfolio Mindsets:

There are 3 approaches designers might take when building their portfolio:

1. ❌ Dumping Projects (no curation), without any though behind the design of the case study or it’s quality
2. ❓ Listing Case Studies (neutral, no message)
3. ✅ Strategic Positioning (tailored, clear, professional)

## What Employers Want To See:

- See all your UXUI skills being applied
- How you solve problems and under what context
- Is there a clear design process?
- UX design principles
- Soft skills
- Hard skills

A portfolio should also be well-written and free of typos, this is really a turn-off to hiring managers. It should be easy to navigate with good information hierarchy without getting lost within the project.

## What to Include in Your Portfolio:

1. Hero section (value prop)
2. About Me
3. 2-4 Case Studies
4. Contact / Socials
5. Testimonials from clients or people you worked with

## Who are you as a designer:

### Designer Types:

1. **UX Designer:** Research-heavy, process-driven, focused on problem framing, user flows, and testing.
2. **UX Researcher:** Understanding users deeply; eg: Interviews, surveys, usability testing, synthesis.
3. **UI Designer:** visual storyteller, detail-obsessed, driven by hierarchy, spacing, typography, colors and interaction.
4. **Product Designer:** End-to-end ownership, business-oriented, often embedded in cross-functional teams.
5. **Generalist:** Broad range of skills across UX, UI, research, and content. Can handle a project from ideation to delivery.
6. **T-Shaped Designer:** Generalist across UX/UI with one area of deep expertise (e.g., accessibility, systems).
7. **Unicorn Designer:** Highly skilled in both UX + UI, and sometimes code (rare and not always sustainable).
8. **Specialist Designers:** Accessibility, Motion, Systems, Content/UX Writing (important in mature companies).

# Portfolio Structure

## A typical structure for a portfolio:

- **About me section (a short sentence pitching yourself)**
    - A trick here is, if any other designer can use the same exact sentence, is not tailored enough to you. _(e.g: I’m a UXUI designer that loves to solve problems to users and companies)_
- **Case Studies (4-6)**
    - Remember, less is more. One very well tailored case study for a company you are applying will have much more impact than 100 unrelated case studies. (also don’t add 100 case studies, cap it at 6)
- **Contact Page**
    - This is a no brainer, they need to contact you somehow.
    - Add your email, linkedin and consider adding socials if those won’t compromise you in any way. Add them only if they add to your advantage, nobody wants to see you drunk during new years eve.
- **CV/Resume**
    - Make this one very visible, and downloadable. Don’t hide it, hiring managers bounce between Linkedin, CV the whole time. Make sure they both match!
        
    - **More info regarding CV/Resumes**
        
        # Why do you think companies open job positions?
        
        They need someone that solves their problem, some of their problems can be:
        
        - Company Growth and Scaling
        - Strategic Restructuring or Reorganization
        - Managing an Increased Workload
        - Internal Promotions Creating Vacancies
        - Short-Term Needs or Project-Based Work
        
        ## What’s the common meta-problem?
        
        **They are understaffed and don’t have time:**
        
        Companies most probably start hiring people when they are understaffed, however, the process is often lengthy and takes a lot of time until the right candidate is hired.
        
        ## ❌ Things to Avoid in your Resume:
        
        ### 1. Presenting Your Work with a job description:
        
        <aside> 💡 Don’t describe the task as ”Delivered a Rebranding of the app”, instead, articulate the Impact “Executed a comprehensive app rebranding strategy,leading to a 25% growth in user acquisition and a significantimprovement in brand sentiment scores across app stores..”
        
        </aside>
        
        ### 2. Ignoring ATS:
        
        Don’t do Visual-First Résumés Skill Sliders & Graphics: Using bars, stars, or percentage circles to rate your skills. also avoid designing in Figma/Photoshop.
        
        ### 3. Show, Don't Tell:
        
        Don’t use empty Self-Praise (it cringy) “One man army”, "Creative Ninja", "Design Guru", "Talented Unicorn," "Jack of all Trades". These are clichés that signal a lack of professional maturity.
        
        ### 4. Consider your reader: Prioritize Scannability
        
        Don’t bury Achievements in a Wall of Text, instead, guide the Eye with Action-Oriented Bullet Points:
        
        - Identified core user frustrations by analyzing heatmaps...
        - Designed and A/B tested three distinct user flows...
        - Delivered a high-fidelity prototype...
        
        ### 5. Replace Fluff with Factual Evidence
        
        Don’t make Vague, Subjective Claims “I’m a good communicator”. Instead Describe the action & the result.Secured full VP approval and budget by presenting a design rationale that clearly tied UX improvements to business goals.
        
        ### 6. Merging formal education With mini courses (specially online)
        
        Don’t misrepresent your educationSome candidates are using of including names like Harvard to make it appears as higher than a course.
        
        <aside> 💡 Example:
        
        EDUCATION ❌:
        
        - Harvard University | 2024
            - UX/UI Design
        - University of Berlin | 2018 - 2022
            - B.A. in Communication Design
        
        EDUCATION ✅:
        
        - University of Berlin | 2018 - 2022
            - B.A. in Communication Design
        - CERTIFICATIONS
            - UX/UI Design Certificate | May 2024 HarvardX (edX Online Platform) </aside>
        
        ## ✅ What to include:
        
        ### 1. Use the XYZ formula:
        
        - Accomplished [X]
        - measured by [Y]
        - by doing [Z].
        
        <aside> 💡 Example:
        
        "Achieved rapid market adoption for a new crypto wallet [X], as measured by 100K user registrations in the first two weeks [Y], by leading a hyper-focused 1-week workshop to define the product strategy, UX, and a 3-month execution plan [Z]."
        
        </aside>
        
        ### 2. Each Application is a Design Problem
        
        Don’t use a One-Size-Fits-All Résumé, generic documents forces the HM to connect your skills to their needs, instead, tailor Your Résumé to Mirror the Job Description the job description is your cheat sheet. Use it to your advantage.
        
        ### 3. Quantify Your Impact Honestly
        
        Numbers are the most powerful way to show your value. But your integrity is your most valuable asset. Never invent data.
        
        - Good: When You Have No Metrics. Use strong, qualitative language to describe the outcome.
        - Better: When You Have Specific Metrics. Lead with the data. The numbers speak for themselves. "Super App designs led to +56% conversion of the peak mini-app.
        - Best: When You Tie Metrics to Business Value. Provide context to show you understand the "why" behind the numbers. "Drove a 56% conversion uplift in our highest-performing mini-app, establishing it as a primary new revenue stream for the business."
        
        ### 4. Prioritize Working experience over education
        
        Always place your experience over titles, certificates or other education.
        
        Don’t: use Weak, Passive Language "Was responsible for the checkout redesign." instead, Lead with a Strong, Specific Action Verb "Redesigned the entire checkout flow, reducing cart abandonment by 15%."

# Portfolio Strategy Scenarios

## Scenario: The visual Designer

I come from a Graphic Design background. I’ve worked for years on visual branding, print,

packaging, & campaigns. Now I’m shifting into UX/UI.

Should I include my old work or not?

## A. Full Reset - Only include UX/UI projects

### Recommended to:

- Career switchers aiming for UX roles in large companies with clearly defined responsibilities.
- Those with enough UX/UI projects to show process, decision-making, and results.

### Full Reset: Pros

- **Sends a clear message:** “I’m a UX/UI designer now.” No distractions
- **Increases focus:** Avoids perception of “adding UX on top” of your graphic design work.

### Full Reset: Cons

- You risk underselling your strongest current skill (visual design), which is highly valued in many jobs.
- Doesn’t reflect your real range — which can be a strong asset in startups and hybrid roles.

## B. Blend the two - UX Case Studies + Visual Gallery

### Recommended to:

- Candidates applying to startups, design-forward brands, or UI-first product companies.
- People who want to apply for Product Designer or UI Designer roles, especially those with creative branding overlaps.

### Blend the two: Pros

- Ideal for UI-heavy roles in startups, small teams, or product companies that expect hybrid profiles.
- Attracts recruiters looking for people who can own the product UI, branding, and marketing visuals — especially in startups or scale-ups
- Signals that you’re strategic about design as a whole — not just focused on screens, but also the brand story and tone.

### Blend the two: Cons

- Needs careful framing and separation between UX and visual work (e.g., use tabs, categories, or subpages).
- Risk that non-design hiring managers (like PMs) won’t understand what’s UX vs visual.

## C: Rework old projects - Reframe graphic projects to UI projects

### Recommended to:

- Those needing bridge content while building real UX case studies.
- Candidates applying for visual-heavy or client-facing UI roles, especially marketing-adjacent products (e.g., campaigns, landing pages, apps with strong branding layers).

### Rework old projects: Pros

- Shows creative reuse of past experience and makes your transition look natural and logical.
- If visual projects had any interaction design, layout logic, or consistency work, you can highlight system thinking and user communication.

### Rework old projects: Cons

- You may spend too much time reworking visuals into something they weren’t — instead of creating genuinely new UX projects.
- Risk of over-intellectualizing visuals that weren’t meant to be UX at all.
