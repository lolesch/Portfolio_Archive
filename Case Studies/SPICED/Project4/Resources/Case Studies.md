# Qualities of Memorable Case Studies

- Clarity, minimal fluff with just enough context
- Decisiveness, to show strong justifiable decisions
- Narrative arc, AKA storytelling, struggle → solution → result.
- Authenticity, not overselling, includes what went wrong
- Character, your voice and personality are present

> [!Example]
> 
> During user interviews and behavior tracking, we discovered that 70% of users dropped off when asked to create an account. I proposed a guest checkout option and simplified the form to 4 fields. After testing, completion rates improved by 27%, and user frustration dropped based on follow-up surveys.

## Red Flags

- Generic phrases like “we did user research” with no specifics
- No actual problem solved
- Endless mockups without reflection
- Over-polished language, no personality
- Too long without clarity

## The 20-Second Rule - First Impressions Matter

In 20 seconds, can I tell:
- What the problem was?
- What you did?
- Why it mattered?

## How to structure your case study

A typical structure for a case study *might* be:
- The Hook
- Research
- Sketches and wireframing
- Prototyping and iteration
- Outcomes and lessons learned

### The Hook

The purpose of the hook is to immediately grab attention and entice the reader to read on. Your hook should convince hiring managers they’ve made the right choice by opening your case study.
### Research
By presenting your research and outcomes in your case study, you show that you care about research-driven, user-centered solutions.
Insights are more valuable than screenshots.

### Sketching and Wireframing
Sketches and wireframes demonstrate your understanding of layout concepts, UI elements, and information hierarchy. They also show your thought processes and the journey from ideas and research insights to final prototypes.

### Prototyping and Iteration
Include both mobile and desktop prototypes in context if your design is multi-device. Link to your prototype files, Highlight any usability testing

### Outcomes and Lessons Learned
Include self-reflection and what you have learned to show growth and adaptability

# What is a UXUI Case Study?

![https://huemor.rocks/blog/how-to-create-a-compelling-case-study/](https://s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/secure.notion-static.com/8287d8ee-315e-4067-bd15-5a30407502a4/How-to-write-a-compelling-case-study2x-1.png.webp)

[https://huemor.rocks/blog/how-to-create-a-compelling-case-study/](https://huemor.rocks/blog/how-to-create-a-compelling-case-study/)

### Definition

**In simple terms, a case study is the story of a design project you have worked on.**

A case study is typically written in a highly visual article format, with accompanying text that guides readers through a curated journey. The term "curated" is important here, as the text should be concise and well written. It's an opportunity to showcase what you want potential employers to know about your work on the project.

### Purpose and Importance

Case studies are a UX designer's secret weapon in two ways:

- First, they can help you get your foot in the door by showcasing your work more effectively than a resume or a top **UX CV** ever could.
- Another benefit is that they're really useful during job interviews. If someone asks about a past project, you can walk them through the case study you've already created.

# Problems of a T**ypical UX case study**

### **The X-ray of a typical UX Case Study**

**Lack of context**

Many designers begin with an unexplained "user need" that assumes a personal need (e.g. "an easier way to share music on Spotify"). User research is then used to attempt to prove that other people share the same problem.

**Too much focus on the process**

Due to the pressure on junior designers to check boxes on a standard design process template, they often forget to explain why they are using a particular method. If everyone's process is somewhat the same, what is your unique approach to the way you work?

**Disconnected steps**

Because of the repetitive nature of the case study format, the different steps of the design process often fail to connect with one another. Insights gathered from one step are rarely applied to the next one. As a result, most case studies feel complete, but few feel innovative.

**Underwhelming solutions**

Many case studies describe the project process in great detail, only to conclude with a predictable, unpolished, and/or unenlightening solution. While the case study can be a great platform to showcase innovation and creativity, many case studies end on an unimpressive note.

### **Summary**

**Uniformity in Design Education**

The emphasis on minimizing time-to-job metrics has led to the standardization of design curricula. This trend is reflected in the types of case studies being produced.

**Lack of Diversity in Design Portfolios**

To increase their chances of finding jobs quickly, designers tend to showcase website and app redesigns in their portfolios. As a result, these types of projects dominate the case studies being published.

**Unquestioned Choice of Platform**

Designers often publish their work on sites like Dribble or Medium, without considering whether senior designers and hiring managers use these platforms.

**Trying to Be All Things to All People**

Many case studies lack a user-centered approach. Designers often create a single version of a case study to appeal to different audiences and achieve diverse goals:

- **For instructors:** Demonstrating that the student has learned the method.
- **For other designers:** Garnering likes and shares on social media platforms like Medium and Dribbble/Behance.
- **For students themselves:** Building a portfolio of work.
- **For recruiters:** Demonstrating that the student is qualified and has practical experience in the field.
- **For hiring managers:** Differentiating the student from other candidates vying for the same position.

# Tips for your case study

![https://www.careerstrategylab.com/how-to-write-a-ux-case-study/](https://s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/secure.notion-static.com/e0c84b8c-0276-466e-bfdc-4ada4c9915b5/Untitled.png)

[https://www.careerstrategylab.com/how-to-write-a-ux-case-study/](https://www.careerstrategylab.com/how-to-write-a-ux-case-study/)

### **Develop Critical Thinking Skills**

More important than following the same steps in the right order every time is the ability to understand when and how each method should be used, and why it will help the designer come up with a better solution to a problem. Designers who can think for themselves will make better decisions when they face their first real project.

### **Avoid Obsessing Over the Process**

Teaching someone to always follow the same steps forms a habit that is hard to break and fosters the misconception that if any of the predefined steps are forgotten, the process is wrong.

### **Design Industry is Not Structured**

Case study formulas create the illusion that real projects will always follow those same predetermined steps. In reality, working with both in-house and agency teams is too chaotic to apply a one-size-fits-all methodology.

### **Improvise!**

When joining a real design team and dealing with real-world briefs and projects, designers need the ability to improvise. Research budgets are not always available, and user journeys are not always necessary. The ability to improvise is one of the skills that differentiates good designers from great ones.

### **The "Dribbblization" of Case Studies**

[Dribbble](https://dribbble.com/) is a powerful platform for community building, but many people post shots there with the sole intention of getting likes from other designers. Case studies on [Medium](https://medium.com/) have started to take a similar path. Discussions tend to be shallow and focused only on positive reinforcement, to the exclusion of critical feedback.

### **Know Your Audience**

A single case study can only speak to so many audiences. If a designer's main objective in creating a case study is to get a foot in the industry, it might be a good idea for them to prioritize recruiters and hiring managers when thinking about their public-facing output. Ultimately, recruiters are the ones who screen candidates, and hiring managers are the ones who make the final decision.

# How to write a case study

[https://youtu.be/jt1WroGHvBs](https://youtu.be/jt1WroGHvBs)

### **Define Your Area of Focus**

The structure of your case study should reflect the areas that interest you the most, as well as those that will help you achieve your personal goals. Consider the type of company you want to work with and craft a story that will excite the design leadership of that organization about the possibility of working with you.

**Focus on the steps of the process that interest you the most** — for example, user research, strategy, UX writing, UI design, prototyping, etc.

**Use language that will appeal to the company you want to attract** — for instance, if you are applying for a UX researcher position, terms like "ethnographic study" will earn you extra points.

**Promote your strongest skills**. If UI design is one of your strengths, avoid showing personas that are shallow or unresearched, as this can hurt you more than it can help.

### **Align Your Story with the Medium**

Before sharing your case study, consider whether it's a good fit for Medium or if it would be better suited for your portfolio website. Will your case study be helpful to others who find it while browsing the internet? Have you ever read another designer's case study in your free time?

Shaping your story to match the platform on which it will be shared is important for making your work feel relevant and engaging. You may need to create multiple versions of the study, tailoring it slightly to the medium.

**On your portfolio,** focus on your design process and output to showcase your skills as a designer.

**On Medium,** focus on insights that other designers can apply to their own work. Medium is a place where designers go to learn new things, not to recruit other designers.

**On Dribbble/Behance,** highlight your most exciting UI moments since these platforms aren't intended for telling longer stories.

**On LinkedIn,** focus on the business impact of your project to showcase your relevance and contributions to the industry.

### **Setting up the Project Context**

When writing about a real project for a client, it's essential to explain the constraints and limitations that affected your work. If there were no such restrictions, as in the case of a project completed for a UX course, hiring managers will expect the designs to be as innovative as possible. They may be frustrated if they're not.

To inform hiring managers' evaluation of your entire study, make sure to address the following questions in your work:

- **Was this a school assignment or a real-world project?**
- **What was the brief? Were you assigned to do a full redesign, or just to add a new feature to an existing product?**
- **What was the team like, and what was your role in the project?**
- **Were there any technical constraints or business KPIs you had to keep in mind while you were executing the assignment?**

### **Focus on Insights Rather than Process**

Everyone's process is somewhat the same. The steps in your process are merely a framework to support your story - they are not the story itself. Reading an insightful case study is much more exciting than reading a complete but uninspired one.

**Highlight what you learned while working on each step,** and explain how you brought those insights to each subsequent step in the process.

**Ensure that your insights are interesting and easy to understand.** Would someone feel compelled to retweet a random sentence from your case study?

**Discuss the things that didn't go well,** and explain how the lessons learned from those experiences made you and your team smarter.

**Teach readers something they don't already know.** Everyone understands the importance of user research in the design process, but not everyone knows that Gen Z users prefer their phone camera to default to selfie mode.

### Design Your Case Study Reading Experience

Put yourself in the shoes of a hiring manager (or someone you would like to impress) who has limited time to read your case study. Make sure your case study is easy to digest and scroll through. The design of your case study page has an enormous impact on how it will be consumed and perceived. Ultimately, reading your case study should feel like flipping through a magazine feature.

**Use Scannable Headings.** Knowing that people will rarely read every word on the page, think about how you can create a coherent story with your headings alone.

**Use Short Sentences and Paragraphs.** Once you’ve written the first version of your case study, rewrite it to make it shorter. Then rewrite it again. And again. And again.

**Write Your Story for Someone Who is Hearing About Your Project and Company for the First Time.** You may have been immersed in that particular business vertical for weeks or even months, but your audience is going to need some context.

**Replace Text with Visuals.** As the old adage goes, “A picture is worth a thousand words.”

### **Obsess Over Your Case Study Visuals**

Being a designer focused on UX rather than UI doesn’t give you permission to make your case study anything less than beautiful and pleasant to look at. A visually impressive case study not only shows off your design skills but, more importantly, communicates the fact that you care about your work.

**Beautiful work will get you interviews. Strategic work will land you the job.**

**Only show the deliverables you are proud of.** Believe us: you don’t have to publish every single file in your project folder.

**Re-create your deliverables.** Now that the project is over, redesign your deliverables so that they look better, are all consistent throughout the case study, and effectively communicate the point you want to get across.

**Use large images and visuals.** Small thumbnails make it difficult for the reader to evaluate the details of your work, and by using undersized images, you miss an opportunity to highlight your craft.

**Include prototypes and motion.** If a picture is worth a thousand words, a prototype is worth a thousand static artboards.

### **Personalize it**

A case study should not only be about the project itself, but also tell a story about how you think and how you design. Ideally, your case study should reflect your personality so strongly that it would feel out of place in any other designer’s portfolio.

**Show why you care.** What excited you the most about working on this project when you first received the brief? What aspects of the process gave you chills while working on it?

**Speak in your own terms.** Sometimes designers are so concerned about using corporate lingo and sounding strategic that they forget to allow their own voice to come through in their writing.

**State your opinions.** Projects designed by someone with a clear vision tend to be more interesting to read. Don’t try to please every reader. For example, hiring managers are looking for people who have a strong point of view on the challenge at hand.

### Treat **case study as a digital product**

Apply the principles of user experience and design to create a well-crafted, engaging, and informative document that is easily accessible and navigable online. This can include using multimedia, interactive elements, and visual design to enhance the user's experience and understanding of the case study.

- Work in segmentation (Chunking)
    - divide a larger piece of information into smaller, more manageable units or "chunks."
- Start with a base MVP
    - create a minimum viable product with essential features that can be built upon in future iterations or versions.
- Slowly expand from there
    - start with a small or basic idea, and then gradually build upon it or add more complexity over time.
- Most important is that it tells a tangible story of your work
    - make sure that your document, project, or presentation conveys a clear and concrete narrative about your work.

This text is discussing a few key points in relation to a project. It suggests working on segmentation (chunking), starting with a minimum viable product (MVP) and gradually expanding from there. The most important aspect is that it tells a tangible story of the work being done.

### Storytelling in your case-study

Use the [STAR method]([https://medium.com/filterdigital/3-steps-to-a-design-portfolio-that-tells-your-story-911df260d5fc#:~:text=If you're not already,static artifacts into compelling narratives.)](https://medium.com/filterdigital/3-steps-to-a-design-portfolio-that-tells-your-story-911df260d5fc#:~:text=If%20you're%20not%20already,static%20artifacts%20into%20compelling%20narratives.\)) to make sure that your case studies & portfolio tell the right story. The STAR method is a structured approach to storytelling used in case studies and portfolios. It stands for **Situation**,**Task, Action,** and **Result**. By using this method, you can ensure that your case studies and portfolio effectively communicate your skills and achievements in a clear and compelling way.

- **Situation** - problem statement, current solution, hypothesis, etc.
- **Task** - research insights, HMW, design scope / plan / roadmap, etc.
- **Action** - design done, process & visuals, step-by-step, wireframes, prototype, etc.
- **Result** - what changed, solution, test, iteration, if given more time, etc.

# 🌈 Further Tips

### Tip 1: Build off a Design Thinking Foundation

Using Design Thinking, we can solve problems from a human-centered perspective. No matter how complex the problem, employing Design Thinking principles can lead to solutions for just about anything.

When writing your case study, consider the elements of Design Thinking that contributed to the design.

<aside> ⚠️ **Attention:** Don’t put constraints on your case study (or your product) by assuming that the Design Thinking process must happen in a linear order or contain all the stages.

</aside>

Were you involved in the entire process? Did other teams handle research or visual design? In most cases, you won’t complete the entire Design Thinking process in a linear fashion, where one step follows the next.

**Think about Your UX Process**

As you write, discuss the Design Thinking methodologies that you used to build your product.

Your case study will have balance, order, and provide the reader with a clear understanding if you walk them through _**your**_ Design Thinking process.

- **Empathize:** Discuss your assumptions, interviews, and research. What steps did you take and why did you take them to understand and genuinely listen to your users?
- **Define:** How did you combine your research and compile your findings? How did you synthesize your findings into a persona and articulate their challenges? Where does the real problem exist?
- **Ideate:** Don’t be afraid to talk about all the fun, crazy, and “ah-ha!” moments you had along the way. All ideas are worthy. How did you prioritize them? How did you explore new and creative ideas?
- **Prototype:** What mockups did you build to learn from your ideas? How did you iterate on them? How did you represent your ideas in tangible form?
- **Test:** Did you roleplay with anyone to test ideas? What worked? What didn’t? What steps did you take to go to your users and test your designs with them? How did you refine and improve?

### Tip 2: Tell a Story

The design process is a journey that goes from an abstract idea to a real product. Often, this journey is full of challenges. When writing a case study, your goal is to tell this story.

To tell a story, find your own writing style. Consider the elements of a good story and practice, practice, practice.

As you write your case study, answer these questions:

- Who is your audience?
- What style and tone should you use?
- What is the theme?
- What is the plot?
- What was the setting?
- Who were the characters?
- What were the conflicts?
- What were the resolutions?

### Tip 3: Be Human

Your case study should reflect your personality and tone. If I meet you and the person in front of me doesn't match the case study you wrote, it becomes quite obvious.

You're not a robot (at least not yet), so don't write like one. If it's appropriate, have some fun. If you need to be serious, do that, but put some human personality into your case study to make it feel genuine.

### Tip 4: Give it Structure

Even the most complex, well-told stories can be broken down into very simple parts. Don't let the structure overwhelm you. Use storytelling methodologies like the three-act structure for basic continuity. If you want to add some spice, integrate dramatic questions into your story and structure.

You won't always use every UX principle for every UX project, and that's okay. But always write about the user, their pain points, and how you solved them.

**Include Relevant Images**

Include relevant pictures as appropriate. Show your deliverables, but don't overload the case study with imagery. It's about the UX process and outcomes, not a Dribbble shot. The reader needs a good balance of both.

If a potential employer were to scan your case study, would they understand what you built? If they took the time to read it, would they get lost in your long paragraphs? Find ways to break up your case study with relevant images, but don't overdo it.

### Tip 5: Look for "I and We"

If you're working in the industry or collaborating on a UX project, it's likely that you're working in a group or on a team. These settings are a great way to learn how to communicate, collaborate, challenge ideas, and learn to give and receive feedback.

**So, what was your role?**

How did you contribute to the product and design process? What particular skills or traits did you bring to the design? Consider areas where you can let the reader know what your specific contributions were to the product by saying "I" more without forgetting your team. Give credit where it's due.

### Tip 6: Finish Strong!

Reflect on the lessons you learned during the design process. What would you have done differently if given more time? Did you launch the product? What metrics did you achieve through your design? What were the outcomes? How has someone's life improved because of your product or design?

### Tip 7: Read it Aloud

Grab a device, like your phone, laptop, or microphone, and record yourself reading your case study out loud. Play it back and listen to yourself.

You'll likely find that what you wrote is never exactly how you would say it in person. Ask yourself if what you hear on the recording sounds like you.

Reading it aloud and recording yourself will also help when it comes time to interview for your next job. You will have already rehearsed your case study. You'll feel more comfortable telling the story and be able to deliver it powerfully in an interview setting.

### Tip 8: Document EVERYTHING!

When you begin a project, take pictures of whiteboards and sketches, keep notes, and continually record your progress along the way. Don't wait until the project is done to start writing.

When you write, your words will "come back to you as a gift." Meaning, the work you put in now to write about your work will allow you to speak better about it, recall certain aspects of the work in conversations, and allow you to fluently walk people through your designs.
